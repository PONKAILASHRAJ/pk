Stopped Martingales and Optional Stopping

Adaptedness, Stopped Filtrations, Regularization, and the Optional Stopping Theorem in Continuous Time
Last updated: June 27, 2025  ·  Stochastic Processes

1. Background and Conventions

We work throughout on a fixed probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ carrying a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}$.

Definition 1 — Usual conditions
The filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$ satisfies the usual conditions if:
  1. Right-continuity: $\mathcal{F}_t = \bigcap_{s>t}\mathcal{F}_s$ for all $t\ge0$.
  2. Completeness: $\mathcal{F}_0$ (hence every $\mathcal{F}_t$) contains all $\mathbb{P}$-null sets of $\mathcal{F}$.
Right-continuity ensures dyadic approximations of stopping times are themselves stopping times. Completeness ensures measurability issues from a.s. equalities are harmless.
Definition 2 — Stopping time and stopped $\sigma$-algebra
A random variable $\tau:\Omega\to[0,\infty]$ is a stopping time with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ if $\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$ for every $t\ge0$. Under right-continuity of the filtration this is equivalent to $\{\tau < t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$ for all $t$.

The stopped $\sigma$-algebra is \[ \mathcal{F}_\tau := \bigl\{A\in\mathcal{F} : A\cap\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t\ \text{ for all }t\ge0\bigr\}. \] Intuitively, $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is the information available to an observer who watches the process up to the random time $\tau$.

Definition 3 — RCLL martingale
A process $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ is an RCLL martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ if:
  1. Martingale property: $\mathbb{E}[|M_t|]<\infty$ for each $t\ge0$ and $\mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]=M_s$ a.s. for all $0\le s\le t$.
  2. RCLL paths: For $\mathbb{P}$-a.e. $\omega$, the sample path $t\mapsto M_t(\omega)$ is right-continuous and has left limits everywhere.
Definition 4 — Stopped process
Given a process $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ and a stopping time $\tau$, the stopped process $M^\tau=(M^\tau_t)_{t\ge0}$ is defined by \[ M^\tau_t := M_{t\wedge\tau}, \qquad t\ge0. \] The stopped process "freezes" at the value $M_\tau$ once time reaches $\tau$.
Remark — Why RCLL?
RCLL is the minimal path regularity that makes $M_\tau$ well-defined and $\mathcal{F}$-measurable for a general stopping time $\tau$. Every $L^1$-bounded martingale in continuous time admits an RCLL modification under the usual conditions.

2. The Stopped $\sigma$-Algebra: Size and Structure

Question 1
Is $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ bigger than $\mathcal{F}_t$ for a fixed time $t$? If $\tau = t$ is the constant stopping time, do we recover $\mathcal{F}_t$? And if $\tau \ge t$ always, what is the relationship between $\mathcal{F}_t$ and $\mathcal{F}_\tau$?
Lemma 1 — Basic properties of $\mathcal{F}_\tau$
Let $\sigma,\tau$ be stopping times with $\sigma\le\tau$ a.s. Then:
  1. $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is indeed a $\sigma$-algebra.
  2. If $\tau = t$ (constant), then $\mathcal{F}_\tau = \mathcal{F}_t$.
  3. $\mathcal{F}_\sigma \subseteq \mathcal{F}_\tau$ whenever $\sigma\le\tau$ a.s.
  4. For any stopping time $\tau$, $\mathcal{F}_\tau\subseteq\mathcal{F}$.
  5. $\{\tau\le s\}\in\mathcal{F}_{\tau}$ and $\{\tau\le s\}\in\mathcal{F}_s$ for every $s\ge0$; in particular $\tau$ is $\mathcal{F}_\tau$-measurable.
Proof

(i) $\Omega\in\mathcal{F}_\tau$ since $\Omega\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$. Closed under complements: if $A\in\mathcal{F}_\tau$, then $A^c\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le t\}\setminus(A\cap\{\tau\le t\})\in\mathcal{F}_t$. Closed under countable unions: $(\bigcup_n A_n)\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\bigcup_n(A_n\cap\{\tau\le t\})\in\mathcal{F}_t$.

(ii) $A\in\mathcal{F}_\tau$ iff $A\cap\{\tau\le s\}\in\mathcal{F}_s$ for all $s\ge0$. For $s<t$: $A\cap\emptyset=\emptyset\in\mathcal{F}_s$ (automatic). For $s\ge t$: $A\cap\Omega=A\in\mathcal{F}_s$ iff $A\in\mathcal{F}_t\subseteq\mathcal{F}_s$. So $\mathcal{F}_\tau=\mathcal{F}_t$.

(iii) Let $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$. For any $t\ge0$, on $\{\sigma>t\}$, since $\sigma\le\tau$, we have $\tau\ge\sigma>t$, so $\{\sigma>t\}\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\emptyset$. Therefore $A\cap\{\tau\le t\}=A\cap\{\sigma\le t\}\cap\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$.

(iv) Immediate from the definition.

(v) Check $\{\tau\le s\}\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le s\wedge t\}\in\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge t}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$. So $\{\tau\le s\}\in\mathcal{F}_\tau$, which implies $\tau$ is $\mathcal{F}_\tau$-measurable. $\square$

Question 2
The stopped $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ encodes "all information up to the random time $\tau$." Intuitively it should contain more than $\mathcal{F}_t$ for any fixed $t$, since $\tau$ is random. But how much bigger is it precisely?
Example — Strict inclusion $\mathcal{F}_t \subsetneq \mathcal{F}_\tau$
Let $B=(B_t)_{t\ge0}$ be a standard Brownian motion with its natural filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, and let \[ \tau := \inf\{t\ge0: B_t = 1\} \] be the first hitting time of $1$. Then $\tau$ is a stopping time and $\tau<\infty$ a.s.

The event $A:=\{\tau\le 1\}=\{\max_{0\le t\le 1}B_t\ge 1\}$ satisfies:

  • $A\in\mathcal{F}_\tau$: indeed $A\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le\min(t,1)\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$ for all $t$.
  • $A\notin\mathcal{F}_t$ for any $t<1$: knowing the path up to a fixed time $t<1$ does not determine whether $B$ will hit $1$ by time $1$.
Thus $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is strictly larger than any fixed-time $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}_t$ with $t<\sup\tau$.
Lemma 2 — Characterisation of $\mathcal{F}_\tau$
Under the usual conditions, for any stopping time $\tau\le N$, \[ \mathcal{F}_\tau = \sigma\bigl(\{M_\tau : M\text{ is an RCLL adapted process}\}\bigr). \] That is, $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra making $X_\tau$ measurable for every RCLL adapted process $X$.
Proof (Sketch)
The inclusion $\supseteq$ follows from measurability of $\omega\mapsto X_{\tau(\omega)}(\omega)$ for any RCLL adapted $X$: for $B\in\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, \[ \{X_\tau\in B\}\cap\{\tau\le t\} = \bigcap_{k=1}^\infty \bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty}\{X_{(j-1)2^{-k}}\in B\}\cap \{(j-1)2^{-k}\le\tau\le j2^{-k}\le t\} \in\mathcal{F}_t, \] using RCLL to approximate $X_\tau$ by dyadic samplings. The inclusion $\subseteq$ follows by taking $X_t:=\mathbf{1}_A\cdot\mathbf{1}_{[t_0,\infty)}(t)$ for $A\in\mathcal{F}_{t_0}$. $\square$

3. Adaptedness of the Stopped Process

Question 3
Is the stopped process $M^\tau_t = M_{t\wedge\tau}$ adapted? We have two natural candidates:
  1. The original filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$.
  2. The stopped filtration $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})_{t\ge0}$.
Also: is $M_\tau$ measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_\tau$?
Lemma 3 — Stopped process is adapted to the original filtration
Let $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ be an RCLL process adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$, and let $\tau$ be a stopping time. Then the stopped process $M^\tau_t = M_{t\wedge\tau}$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable for every $t\ge0$.
Proof
Fix $t\ge0$. Write \[ M_{t\wedge\tau} = M_t\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau\ge t\}} + M_\tau\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau<t\}}. \]

First term: $M_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable by adaptedness, and $\{\tau\ge t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$.

Second term: Approximate $\tau$ from above by the dyadic sequence $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$. Then \[ M_{\tau_n}\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau_n<t\}} = \sum_{k:\,k\cdot2^{-n}<t} M_{k\cdot2^{-n}}\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau_n=k\cdot2^{-n}\}} \] is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable. Since $M$ has RCLL paths and $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$, we get $M_{\tau_n}\to M_\tau$ a.s., so the limit is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable by completeness. $\square$

Lemma 4 — Stopped process is adapted to the stopped filtration
Under the same hypotheses, $M^\tau_t = M_{t\wedge\tau}$ is also $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$-measurable for every $t\ge0$.
Proof
Since $t\wedge\tau$ is a stopping time, $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$ is well-defined. It suffices to show $M_\sigma$ is $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable for any stopping time $\sigma$ (here $\sigma = t\wedge\tau$).

Dyadic approximation. Define $\sigma_n := \lceil 2^n\sigma\rceil/2^n\wedge N$. Then $M_{\sigma_n} = \sum_k M_{k2^{-n}}\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\sigma_n = k2^{-n}\}}$ is $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable by checking each indicator lies in $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$.

Limit. Since $M$ has RCLL paths and $\sigma_n\downarrow\sigma$, $M_{\sigma_n}\to M_\sigma$ a.s. The limit of $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable functions is $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable by completeness. $\square$

Question 4
We have shown $(M^\tau_t)$ is adapted to both $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})$ and $(\mathcal{F}_t)$. Since $t\wedge\tau\le t$, Lemma 1(iii) gives $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$. So the stopped filtration is the coarser one. But can the inclusion be strict?
Lemma 5 — Stopped filtration is coarser than the original
For any stopping time $\tau$ and any $t\ge0$:
  1. $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$.
  2. In general, the inclusion is strict when $\tau<t$ with positive probability.
  3. On the event $\{\tau\ge t\}$, the two coincide.
Proof

(i) Immediate from Lemma 1(iii) since $t\wedge\tau\le t$ always.

(ii) Take $B=(B_t)$ standard Brownian motion, $\tau=\inf\{t:B_t=1\}$, and fix $t=2$. The event $A=\{B_2>2\}\in\mathcal{F}_2$ but $A\notin\mathcal{F}_{2\wedge\tau}$, since on $\{\tau\le 2\}$ the process has been stopped and $B_2$ is not determined by the stopped path.

(iii) On $\{\tau\ge t\}$, we have $t\wedge\tau=t$, so $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}=\mathcal{F}_t$ there. $\square$

Remark — Intuition on the two filtrations
$\mathcal{F}_t$ records everything up to time $t$, even information about the process after $\tau$. The stopped filtration $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$ records only information up to $\min(t,\tau)$ — it genuinely "forgets" about later times once the process has been stopped. This makes $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$ the more intrinsic filtration for the stopped process.

4. The Stopped Martingale is a Martingale

Let $M_t$ be a martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$. We ask three questions.

Question 5(a)
Is $(M^\tau_t)=(M_{t\wedge\tau})$ a martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$? Does it satisfy \[ \mathbb{E}\bigl[M^\tau_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s\bigr] = M^\tau_s = M_{s\wedge\tau} \quad\text{a.s., for all }0\le s\le t\,? \]
Question 5(b)
Is $(M^\tau_t)$ a martingale with respect to the stopped filtration $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})$? \[ \mathbb{E}\bigl[M^\tau_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}\bigr] = M_{s\wedge\tau} \quad\text{a.s., for all }0\le s\le t\,? \]
Question 5(c)
If $\tau_1, \tau_2$ are two stopping times with $\tau_1\le\tau_2$ a.s., is \[ \mathbb{E}[M_{\tau_2}\mid\mathcal{F}_{\tau_1}] = M_{\tau_1}\,? \] If not, when is it true?

If we prove 5(a), then since the stopped filtration is smaller and the stopped process is adapted to it, the general theorem below implies 5(b) immediately. We call 5(a) the Optional Stopping Theorem.

Theorem 2 — Stopped martingale w.r.t. stopped filtration
Let $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ be an RCLL martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$ satisfying the usual conditions. Let $\tau$ be a bounded stopping time, $\tau\le N$. Then $(M^\tau_t)$ is a martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})_{t\ge0}$: for all $0\le s\le t$,
  1. $M^\tau_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$-measurable and integrable.
  2. $\mathbb{E}\bigl[M^\tau_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}\bigr]=M^\tau_s$ a.s.
Proof

Part (i): Adaptedness was established in Lemma 4. Integrability follows from $\mathbb{E}[|M^\tau_t|] = \mathbb{E}[|M_{t\wedge\tau}|] \le \mathbb{E}[|M_N|] < \infty$ by the Optional Stopping Theorem with $0\le t\wedge\tau\le N$.

Part (ii): Fix $0\le s\le t$. We must show for every $A\in\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}$: \[ \int_A M_{t\wedge\tau}\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A M_{s\wedge\tau}\,d\mathbb{P}. \tag{$\dagger$} \] Set $\sigma := s\wedge\tau$ and $\rho := t\wedge\tau$. Then $\sigma\le\rho$, both bounded by $N$, and $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$. The Optional Stopping Theorem (Theorem 1) gives exactly $(\dagger)$. $\square$

Remark — Stopped martingale w.r.t. original filtration
In fact $(M^\tau_t)$ is also a martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$: for $0\le s\le t$, \[ \mathbb{E}\bigl[M_{t\wedge\tau}\mid\mathcal{F}_s\bigr] = M_{s\wedge\tau} \quad\text{a.s.} \] This follows from the Optional Stopping Theorem with $\sigma := s\wedge\tau\le\rho := t\wedge\tau$ and $A\in\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_s$.
Theorem (Coarser filtration)
Let $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ be a martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, and let $(\mathcal{G}_t)_{t\ge0}$ be a filtration with $\mathcal{G}_t\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$ for all $t\ge0$. Then $(M_t)$ is a martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{G}_t)$ if and only if $(M_t)$ is adapted to $(\mathcal{G}_t)$.
Proof

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $(M_t)$ is a $(\mathcal{G}_t)$-martingale, it is adapted to $(\mathcal{G}_t)$ by definition.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $(M_t)$ is adapted to $(\mathcal{G}_t)$. Since $(M_t)$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_t)$-martingale, $\mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]=M_s$. By the tower property and $\mathcal{G}_s\subseteq\mathcal{F}_s$: \[ \mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{G}_s] = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]\mid\mathcal{G}_s\bigr] = \mathbb{E}[M_s\mid\mathcal{G}_s] = M_s, \] where the last equality uses $\mathcal{G}_s$-measurability of $M_s$. $\square$

5. The Optional Stopping Theorem

Theorem 1 — Optional Stopping (Bounded Case)
Let $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ be an RCLL martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$ satisfying the usual conditions. Let $\sigma\le\rho$ be stopping times bounded by $N<\infty$. Then \[ \mathbb{E}\bigl[M_\rho\mid\mathcal{F}_\sigma\bigr] = M_\sigma \quad\text{a.s.} \] Equivalently, for every $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$: $\displaystyle\int_A M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A M_\sigma\,d\mathbb{P}$.

Proof strategy: (1) Prove for simple stopping times using the martingale property alone. (2) Approximate any bounded stopping time by simple ones via dyadic rounding. (3) Pass to the limit using RCLL (a.s. convergence) and uniform integrability.

Step 1: Simple Stopping Times

Definition 5 — Simple stopping time
A stopping time $\tau$ is simple if it takes only finitely many values: there exist $0\le t_1<\cdots<t_m<\infty$ and a partition $\Omega=\bigsqcup_{i=1}^m B_i$ with $B_i\in\mathcal{F}_{t_i}$ such that $\tau=\sum_i t_i\,\mathbf{1}_{B_i}$.
Lemma 6 — Base case: simple stopping times
Theorem 1 holds when $\sigma$ and $\rho$ are simple stopping times.
Proof

Decompose over values of $\sigma$. Let $\{s_i\}$ be its values, $A_i := A\cap\{\sigma=s_i\}\in\mathcal{F}_{s_i}$. Suffices to show $\int_{A_i}M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_{A_i}M_{s_i}\,d\mathbb{P}$ for each $i$.

Claim
For any $s\le t$ and $B\in\mathcal{F}_s$: $\displaystyle\int_B M_t\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_B M_s\,d\mathbb{P}$. $(*)$

Proof of Claim: The martingale property gives $\mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]=M_s$; multiplying by $\mathbf{1}_B$ and integrating gives $(*)$. $\square$

Let $\{u_j\}$ be the values of $\rho$. Each $C_{ij}:=A_i\cap\{\rho=u_j\}\in\mathcal{F}_{s_i}$ and $u_j\ge s_i$ on $C_{ij}$. Applying $(*)$: \[ \int_{A_i}M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P} = \sum_j\int_{C_{ij}}M_{u_j}\,d\mathbb{P} \overset{(*)}{=} \sum_j\int_{C_{ij}}M_{s_i}\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_{A_i}M_{s_i}\,d\mathbb{P}. \quad\square \]

Step 2: Dyadic Approximation

Construction
For a bounded stopping time $\tau\le N$ and $n\ge1$, set $\tau_n := \frac{\lceil 2^n\tau\rceil}{2^n}\wedge N.$
Lemma 7 — Properties of the dyadic approximation
The sequence $(\tau_n)$ satisfies: (i) $\tau_n$ is a simple stopping time; (ii) $\tau\le\tau_n\le\tau+2^{-n}$; (iii) $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$ pointwise; (iv) $\sigma\le\rho$ implies $\sigma_n\le\rho_n$.

Step 3: Passing to the Limit

By Lemma 6, for each $n$: $\int_A M_{\rho_n}\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A M_{\sigma_n}\,d\mathbb{P}$ for all $A\in\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$. $\quad (**)$

Sub-step 3a — Almost sure convergence  [RCLL used here and only here]
Since $M$ has RCLL paths and $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$, right-continuity gives $M_{\tau_n}(\omega)\to M_{\tau(\omega)}(\omega)$ a.s. Hence $M_{\sigma_n}\to M_\sigma$ and $M_{\rho_n}\to M_\rho$ a.s. This is the unique step using RCLL.
Sub-step 3b — Uniform integrability
Since $\tau_n\le N$, all stopped values lie in $\{M_t : t\le N\}$. This family is UI because $M_t = \mathbb{E}[M_N\mid\mathcal{F}_t]$ and $\{\mathbb{E}[M_N\mid\mathcal{G}]\}_{\mathcal{G}}$ is UI for any $M_N\in L^1$. Hence $M_{\sigma_n}\xrightarrow{L^1}M_\sigma$ and $M_{\rho_n}\xrightarrow{L^1}M_\rho$.
Sub-step 3c — Filtration inclusion
$\sigma\le\sigma_n$ implies $\mathcal{F}_\sigma\subseteq\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$ by Lemma 1(iii). So any $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$ is in $\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$, making $(**)$ applicable.
Conclusion — Completing the proof of Theorem 1
For any $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$: by Sub-step 3c, $A\in\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$, so $(**)$ gives $\int_A M_{\rho_n}\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A M_{\sigma_n}\,d\mathbb{P}$. Taking $n\to\infty$ via $L^1$ convergence: \[ \int_A M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A M_\sigma\,d\mathbb{P}. \] Since $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$ was arbitrary, $\mathbb{E}[M_\rho\mid\mathcal{F}_\sigma]=M_\sigma$ a.s. $\blacksquare$

6. Augmented Filtrations and the Regularization Theorem

Definition 6 — Augmented filtration $\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_\bullet$
Let $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$ be a filtration on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$.
  1. The $\mathbb{P}$-completion of $\mathcal{F}_t$ is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_t := \sigma(\mathcal{F}_t\cup\mathcal{N})$, where $\mathcal{N}$ is the collection of all $\mathbb{P}$-null sets.
  2. The right-continuous augmented filtration is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t := \bigcap_{s>t}\bar{\mathcal{F}}_s$, $t\ge0$.
The filtration $(\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t)_{t\ge0}$ satisfies the usual conditions by construction.
Lemma 8 — RCLL submartingales and augmented filtrations
Suppose $(X_t)_{t\ge0}$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_t)$-submartingale with RCLL paths. Then $(X_t)$ is also a $(\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t)$-submartingale.
Proof

(a) Adaptedness. Since $X$ has RCLL paths, $X_t = \lim_{u\downarrow t}X_u$ a.s. For each $u>t$, $X_u$ is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_u$-measurable, so the limit is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t$-measurable.

(b) Integrability. Unchanged from the submartingale hypothesis.

(c) Conditional inequality. For $A\in\bar{\mathcal{F}}_s$, write $A=B\triangle N$ with $B\in\mathcal{F}_s$ and $N$ null. Then $\int_A X_t\,d\mathbb{P}\ge\int_A X_s\,d\mathbb{P}$ extends from $\mathcal{F}_s$ to $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_s$. For $u\downarrow s$: $\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}_u]\ge X_u$ a.s. By Lévy's backward theorem, $\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}_u]\to\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_s]$ a.s. and $X_u\to X_s$ a.s. by RCLL. Hence $\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_s]\ge X_s$ a.s. $\square$

Theorem 3 — Regularization of martingales
Let $(X_t)_{t\ge0}$ be a martingale w.r.t. a right-continuous filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$, with $X_t\in L^1$ for all $t$. Then there exists a process $Y=(Y_t)_{t\ge0}$ such that:
  1. Modification: $Y_t=X_t$ $\mathbb{P}$-a.s. for every fixed $t\ge0$.
  2. RCLL paths: For $\mathbb{P}$-a.e. $\omega$, $t\mapsto Y_t(\omega)$ is right-continuous with left limits everywhere.
  3. Martingale: $(Y_t)$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_t)$-martingale.
Proof

Step 1: Right-continuous regularization along rationals. For each $t\ge0$, the limit $Y_t := \lim_{s\downarrow t,\,s\in\mathbb{Q}_+}X_s$ exists a.s. and in $L^1$ by UI: since $X_s=\mathbb{E}[X_T\mid\mathcal{F}_s]$ for $s\le T$, the family $\{X_s : s\in\mathbb{Q}_+,\,t\le s\le T\}$ is UI.

Step 2: $Y_t=X_t$ a.s. By right-continuity of $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, for $A\in\mathcal{F}_t$ and $s_n\downarrow t$: $\int_A Y_t\,d\mathbb{P} = \lim_n\int_A X_{s_n}\,d\mathbb{P} = \lim_n\int_A X_t\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A X_t\,d\mathbb{P}$, giving $Y_t=X_t$ a.s.

Step 3: RCLL paths. Right-continuity: by definition, $Y_t = \lim_{u\downarrow t,u\in\mathbb{Q}_+}Y_u$. Left limits: the upcrossing inequality for submartingales along rationals implies sample paths have left limits everywhere a.s.

Step 4: $Y$ is a martingale. For $s\le t$ and $A\in\mathcal{F}_s$: $\int_A Y_t\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A X_t\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A X_s\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A Y_s\,d\mathbb{P}$. $\square$

Remark — Doob's regularization theorem
This is often called Doob's regularization theorem. It is the theoretical backbone of continuous-time stochastic analysis: it justifies the standing assumption that all martingales have RCLL paths, which makes $M_\tau$ well-defined for stopping times $\tau$, enables the Optional Stopping Theorem, and supports the Doob–Meyer decomposition and stochastic integration.
