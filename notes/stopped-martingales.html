Stopped Martingales and Optional Stopping

Adaptedness, Stopped Filtrations, Regularization, and the Optional Stopping Theorem in Continuous Time
Last updated: June 27, 2025  ·  Stochastic Processes

1. Background and Conventions

We work throughout on a fixed probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ carrying a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}$.

Definition 1 — Usual conditions
The filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\ge0}$ satisfies the usual conditions if:
  1. Right-continuity: $\mathcal{F}_t = \bigcap_{s>t}\mathcal{F}_s$ for all $t\ge0$.
  2. Completeness: $\mathcal{F}_0$ (hence every $\mathcal{F}_t$) contains all $\mathbb{P}$-null sets of $\mathcal{F}$.
Right-continuity ensures dyadic approximations of stopping times are themselves stopping times. Completeness ensures measurability issues from a.s. equalities are harmless.
Definition 2 — Stopping time and stopped σ-algebra
A random variable $\tau:\Omega\to[0,\infty]$ is a stopping time w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ if $\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$ for every $t\ge0$.

The stopped σ-algebra is \[ \mathcal{F}_\tau := \bigl\{A\in\mathcal{F} : A\cap\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t\ \text{ for all }t\ge0\bigr\}. \] Intuitively, $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is the information available to an observer who watches the process up to the random time $\tau$.

Definition 3 — RCLL martingale
A process $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ is an RCLL martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ if:
  1. Martingale property: $\mathbb{E}[|M_t|]<\infty$ and $\mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]=M_s$ a.s. for all $0\le s\le t$.
  2. RCLL paths: For $\mathbb{P}$-a.e. $\omega$, the sample path $t\mapsto M_t(\omega)$ is right-continuous with left limits everywhere.
Definition 4 — Stopped process
Given a process $(M_t)_{t\ge0}$ and a stopping time $\tau$, the stopped process is \[ M^\tau_t := M_{t\wedge\tau}, \qquad t\ge0. \] The stopped process "freezes" at the value $M_\tau$ once time reaches $\tau$.
Remark — Why RCLL?
RCLL is the minimal path regularity that makes $M_\tau$ well-defined and $\mathcal{F}$-measurable for a general stopping time $\tau$. Every $L^1$-bounded martingale in continuous time admits an RCLL modification under the usual conditions.

2. The Stopped σ-Algebra: Size and Structure

Question 1
Is $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ bigger than $\mathcal{F}_t$ for a fixed time $t$? If $\tau = t$ is the constant stopping time, do we recover $\mathcal{F}_t$? And if $\tau \ge t$ always, what is the relationship between $\mathcal{F}_t$ and $\mathcal{F}_\tau$?
Lemma 1 — Basic properties of $\mathcal{F}_\tau$
Let $\sigma,\tau$ be stopping times with $\sigma\le\tau$ a.s. Then:
  1. $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is a $\sigma$-algebra.
  2. If $\tau = t$ (constant), then $\mathcal{F}_\tau = \mathcal{F}_t$.
  3. $\mathcal{F}_\sigma \subseteq \mathcal{F}_\tau$ whenever $\sigma\le\tau$ a.s.
  4. $\mathcal{F}_\tau\subseteq\mathcal{F}$ (sub-σ-algebra of the full σ-algebra).
  5. $\{\tau\le s\}\in\mathcal{F}_{\tau}$ for every $s\ge0$; in particular $\tau$ is $\mathcal{F}_\tau$-measurable.
Proof

(i) $\Omega\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$. Closed under complements: $A^c\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le t\}\setminus(A\cap\{\tau\le t\})\in\mathcal{F}_t$. Closed under countable unions: $(\bigcup_n A_n)\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\bigcup_n(A_n\cap\{\tau\le t\})\in\mathcal{F}_t$.

(ii) For $s<t$: $A\cap\emptyset=\emptyset\in\mathcal{F}_s$ automatically. For $s\ge t$: $A\cap\Omega=A\in\mathcal{F}_s$ iff $A\in\mathcal{F}_t\subseteq\mathcal{F}_s$. So $\mathcal{F}_\tau=\mathcal{F}_t$.

(iii) Let $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$. On $\{\sigma>t\}$, since $\sigma\le\tau$, we have $\tau\ge\sigma>t$, so $\{\sigma>t\}\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\emptyset$. Therefore $A\cap\{\tau\le t\}=A\cap\{\sigma\le t\}\cap\{\tau\le t\}\in\mathcal{F}_t$.

(iv) Immediate from the definition. (v) Check $\{\tau\le s\}\cap\{\tau\le t\}=\{\tau\le s\wedge t\}\in\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge t}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$. $\square$

Question 2
How much bigger is $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ than any fixed-time $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}_t$?
Example — Strict inclusion $\mathcal{F}_t \subsetneq \mathcal{F}_\tau$
Let $B=(B_t)_{t\ge0}$ be standard Brownian motion, and let $\tau := \inf\{t\ge0: B_t = 1\}$. Then $\tau<\infty$ a.s. The event $A:=\{\tau\le 1\}=\{\max_{0\le t\le 1}B_t\ge 1\}$ satisfies $A\in\mathcal{F}_\tau$ but $A\notin\mathcal{F}_t$ for any $t<1$. Thus $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is strictly larger than any fixed-time $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}_t$ with $t<\sup\tau$.
Lemma 2 — Characterisation of $\mathcal{F}_\tau$
Under the usual conditions, $\mathcal{F}_\tau = \sigma\bigl(\{M_\tau : M\text{ is RCLL adapted}\}\bigr)$. That is, $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra making $X_\tau$ measurable for every RCLL adapted process $X$.
Proof (Sketch)
The inclusion $\supseteq$ follows from: for $B\in\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, \[ \{X_\tau\in B\}\cap\{\tau\le t\} = \bigcap_{k=1}^\infty \bigcup_{j=1}^{\infty}\{X_{(j-1)2^{-k}}\in B\}\cap \{(j-1)2^{-k}\le\tau\le j2^{-k}\le t\} \in\mathcal{F}_t, \] using RCLL to approximate $X_\tau$ by dyadic samplings. The inclusion $\subseteq$ follows by taking $X_t:=\mathbf{1}_A\cdot\mathbf{1}_{[t_0,\infty)}(t)$ for $A\in\mathcal{F}_{t_0}$. $\square$

3. Adaptedness of the Stopped Process

Question 3
Is the stopped process $M^\tau_t = M_{t\wedge\tau}$ adapted to (i) the original filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, or (ii) the stopped filtration $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})$? Also: is $M_\tau$ measurable w.r.t. $\mathcal{F}_\tau$?
Lemma 3 — Adapted to the original filtration
Let $(M_t)$ be RCLL adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, and $\tau$ a stopping time. Then $M_{t\wedge\tau}$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable for every $t\ge0$.
Proof
Write $M_{t\wedge\tau} = M_t\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau\ge t\}} + M_\tau\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau<t\}}$. The first term is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable. For the second, approximate $\tau$ by dyadic $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$: then $M_{\tau_n}\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau_n<t\}}$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable (finite sum), and $M_{\tau_n}\to M_\tau$ a.s. by RCLL, so the limit is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable by completeness. $\square$
Lemma 4 — Adapted to the stopped filtration
Under the same hypotheses, $M_{t\wedge\tau}$ is also $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$-measurable for every $t\ge0$.
Proof
It suffices to show $M_\sigma$ is $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable for any stopping time $\sigma$ (here $\sigma=t\wedge\tau$). Define $\sigma_n:=\lceil 2^n\sigma\rceil/2^n\wedge N$. Then $M_{\sigma_n}=\sum_k M_{k2^{-n}}\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\sigma_n=k2^{-n}\}}$ is $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable (each indicator lies in $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$ by a direct check). Since $M$ has RCLL paths and $\sigma_n\downarrow\sigma$, $M_{\sigma_n}\to M_\sigma$ a.s., so the limit is $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$-measurable by completeness. $\square$
Question 4
We showed $(M^\tau_t)$ is adapted to both $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})$ and $(\mathcal{F}_t)$. Since $t\wedge\tau\le t$, Lemma 1(iii) gives $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$. So the stopped filtration is coarser. Can the inclusion be strict?
Lemma 5 — Stopped filtration is coarser than the original
For any stopping time $\tau$ and $t\ge0$: (i) $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$; (ii) the inclusion is strict when $\tau<t$ with positive probability; (iii) on $\{\tau\ge t\}$ the two coincide.
Proof

(i) Immediate from Lemma 1(iii). (iii) On $\{\tau\ge t\}$, $t\wedge\tau=t$ so $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}=\mathcal{F}_t$ there.

(ii) Take BM $B_t$, $\tau=\inf\{t:B_t=1\}$, $t=2$. Then $A=\{B_2>2\}\in\mathcal{F}_2$ but $A\notin\mathcal{F}_{2\wedge\tau}$, since on $\{\tau\le 2\}$ the value of $B_2$ is not determined by the stopped path. $\square$

Remark — Intuition on the two filtrations
$\mathcal{F}_t$ records everything up to time $t$, even after $\tau$. The stopped filtration $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$ records only information up to $\min(t,\tau)$ — it genuinely "forgets" later times. This makes $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$ the more intrinsic filtration for the stopped process.

4. The Stopped Martingale is a Martingale

Let $(M_t)$ be a martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$. We ask three questions.

Question 5(a)
Is $(M_{t\wedge\tau})$ a martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$? i.e. does $\mathbb{E}[M^\tau_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = M_{s\wedge\tau}$ a.s. for all $0\le s\le t$?
Question 5(b)
Is $(M_{t\wedge\tau})$ a martingale w.r.t. the stopped filtration $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})$? i.e. does $\mathbb{E}[M^\tau_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}] = M_{s\wedge\tau}$ a.s.?
Question 5(c)
If $\tau_1\le\tau_2$ a.s. are two stopping times, is $\mathbb{E}[M_{\tau_2}\mid\mathcal{F}_{\tau_1}] = M_{\tau_1}$? If not, when is it true?

If we prove 5(a), then since the stopped filtration is smaller and the process is adapted to it, the theorem below implies 5(b) immediately. We call 5(a) the Optional Stopping Theorem.

Theorem (Coarser filtration)
Let $(M_t)$ be a martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, and $(\mathcal{G}_t)$ a filtration with $\mathcal{G}_t\subseteq\mathcal{F}_t$ for all $t$. Then $(M_t)$ is a $(\mathcal{G}_t)$-martingale if and only if it is adapted to $(\mathcal{G}_t)$.
Proof
$(\Rightarrow)$ By definition. $(\Leftarrow)$ By the tower property and $\mathcal{G}_s\subseteq\mathcal{F}_s$: \[ \mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{G}_s] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[M_t\mid\mathcal{F}_s]\mid\mathcal{G}_s] = \mathbb{E}[M_s\mid\mathcal{G}_s] = M_s, \] where the last equality uses $\mathcal{G}_s$-measurability of $M_s$. $\square$
Theorem 2 — Stopped martingale w.r.t. stopped filtration
Let $(M_t)$ be an RCLL martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ satisfying the usual conditions, and $\tau\le N$ a bounded stopping time. Then $(M_{t\wedge\tau})$ is a martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau})$: for all $0\le s\le t$,
  1. $M_{t\wedge\tau}$ is $\mathcal{F}_{t\wedge\tau}$-measurable and integrable.
  2. $\mathbb{E}[M_{t\wedge\tau}\mid\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}]=M_{s\wedge\tau}$ a.s.
Proof

(i) Adaptedness is Lemma 4. Integrability: $\mathbb{E}[|M_{t\wedge\tau}|]\le\mathbb{E}[|M_N|]<\infty$ by OST.

(ii) Set $\sigma:=s\wedge\tau$, $\rho:=t\wedge\tau$. Then $\sigma\le\rho$, both bounded by $N$, and any $A\in\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}=\mathcal{F}_\sigma$ satisfies $\int_A M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A M_\sigma\,d\mathbb{P}$ by the Optional Stopping Theorem (Theorem 1). $\square$

Remark — Stopped martingale w.r.t. original filtration
$(M_{t\wedge\tau})$ is also a martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$: for $0\le s\le t$, $\mathbb{E}[M_{t\wedge\tau}\mid\mathcal{F}_s]=M_{s\wedge\tau}$ a.s. This follows from OST with $\sigma:=s\wedge\tau\le\rho:=t\wedge\tau$ and $A\in\mathcal{F}_{s\wedge\tau}\subseteq\mathcal{F}_s$.

5. The Optional Stopping Theorem

Theorem 1 — Optional Stopping (Bounded Case)
Let $(M_t)$ be an RCLL martingale w.r.t. $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ satisfying the usual conditions. Let $\sigma\le\rho$ be stopping times bounded by $N<\infty$. Then \[ \mathbb{E}\bigl[M_\rho\mid\mathcal{F}_\sigma\bigr] = M_\sigma \quad\text{a.s.} \]

Proof strategy: (1) Prove for simple stopping times. (2) Approximate by dyadic rounding. (3) Pass to the limit via RCLL and uniform integrability.

Step 1: Simple Stopping Times

Definition 5 — Simple stopping time
A stopping time $\tau$ is simple if $\tau=\sum_i t_i\,\mathbf{1}_{B_i}$ for finitely many $t_i$ and $B_i\in\mathcal{F}_{t_i}$ partitioning $\Omega$.
Lemma 6 — Base case: simple stopping times
Theorem 1 holds when $\sigma$ and $\rho$ are simple stopping times.
Proof
Decompose over values $\{s_i\}$ of $\sigma$: set $A_i:=A\cap\{\sigma=s_i\}\in\mathcal{F}_{s_i}$. The martingale property gives $\int_B M_t\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_B M_s\,d\mathbb{P}$ for $B\in\mathcal{F}_s$, $s\le t$. For each $C_{ij}:=A_i\cap\{\rho=u_j\}\in\mathcal{F}_{s_i}$ (with $u_j\ge s_i$): \[ \int_{A_i}M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P} = \sum_j\int_{C_{ij}}M_{u_j}\,d\mathbb{P} = \sum_j\int_{C_{ij}}M_{s_i}\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_{A_i}M_{s_i}\,d\mathbb{P}. \quad\square \]

Step 2: Dyadic Approximation

Construction
For a bounded stopping time $\tau\le N$ and $n\ge1$: $\tau_n := \frac{\lceil 2^n\tau\rceil}{2^n}\wedge N.$ This is simple, $\tau\le\tau_n\le\tau+2^{-n}$, and $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$ pointwise.

Step 3: Passing to the Limit

By Lemma 6: $\int_A M_{\rho_n}\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A M_{\sigma_n}\,d\mathbb{P}$ for all $A\in\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$. $\quad (**)$

Sub-step 3a — Almost sure convergence  [RCLL used here and only here]
Since $M$ has RCLL paths and $\tau_n\downarrow\tau$: $M_{\tau_n}\to M_\tau$ a.s. Hence $M_{\sigma_n}\to M_\sigma$ and $M_{\rho_n}\to M_\rho$ a.s.
Sub-step 3b — Uniform integrability
Since $M_t=\mathbb{E}[M_N\mid\mathcal{F}_t]$ and conditional expectations of a fixed $L^1$ variable are UI, $\{M_t:t\le N\}$ is UI. Hence $M_{\sigma_n}\xrightarrow{L^1}M_\sigma$ and $M_{\rho_n}\xrightarrow{L^1}M_\rho$.
Sub-step 3c — Filtration inclusion
$\sigma\le\sigma_n$ implies $\mathcal{F}_\sigma\subseteq\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$, so any $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$ is in $\mathcal{F}_{\sigma_n}$ and $(**)$ applies.
Conclusion
For any $A\in\mathcal{F}_\sigma$: $(**)$ and $L^1$ convergence give \[ \int_A M_\rho\,d\mathbb{P} = \lim_n\int_A M_{\rho_n}\,d\mathbb{P} = \lim_n\int_A M_{\sigma_n}\,d\mathbb{P} = \int_A M_\sigma\,d\mathbb{P}. \] Since $A$ was arbitrary, $\mathbb{E}[M_\rho\mid\mathcal{F}_\sigma]=M_\sigma$ a.s. $\blacksquare$

6. Augmented Filtrations and Regularization

Definition 6 — Augmented filtration
Let $\mathcal{N}$ be the collection of all $\mathbb{P}$-null sets. The augmented filtration is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_t:=\sigma(\mathcal{F}_t\cup\mathcal{N})$, and the right-continuous augmented filtration is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t:=\bigcap_{s>t}\bar{\mathcal{F}}_s$. The filtration $(\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t)$ satisfies the usual conditions by construction.
Lemma 8 — RCLL submartingales and augmented filtrations
If $(X_t)$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_t)$-submartingale with RCLL paths, then it is also a $(\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t)$-submartingale.
Proof

Adaptedness: $X_t=\lim_{u\downarrow t}X_u$ a.s. by RCLL, and each $X_u$ is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_u$-measurable, so the limit is $\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_t$-measurable.

Inequality: The submartingale inequality extends from $\mathcal{F}_s$ to $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_s$ by null-set arguments. For $u\downarrow s$: $\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}_u]\ge X_u$ a.s. By Lévy's backward theorem $\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}_u]\to\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_s]$ a.s., and $X_u\to X_s$ a.s. by RCLL. Hence $\mathbb{E}[X_t\mid\bar{\mathcal{F}}^+_s]\ge X_s$ a.s. $\square$

Theorem 3 — Regularization (Doob)
Let $(X_t)$ be a martingale w.r.t. a right-continuous filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$, with $X_t\in L^1$. Then there exists a process $(Y_t)$ such that: (i) $Y_t=X_t$ a.s. for every fixed $t$; (ii) $Y$ has RCLL paths a.s.; (iii) $(Y_t)$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_t)$-martingale.
Proof

Step 1: Set $Y_t:=\lim_{s\downarrow t,s\in\mathbb{Q}_+}X_s$. This limit exists a.s. and in $L^1$ by UI (since $X_s=\mathbb{E}[X_T\mid\mathcal{F}_s]$ for $s\le T$).

Step 2: By right-continuity of $(\mathcal{F}_t)$: $\int_A Y_t\,d\mathbb{P}=\lim_n\int_A X_{s_n}\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A X_t\,d\mathbb{P}$ for all $A\in\mathcal{F}_t$, giving $Y_t=X_t$ a.s.

Step 3: Right-continuity of $Y$ follows by definition. Left limits exist a.s. by the upcrossing inequality applied along rationals.

Step 4: $\int_A Y_t\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A X_t\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A X_s\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_A Y_s\,d\mathbb{P}$ for $A\in\mathcal{F}_s$, so $(Y_t)$ is a martingale. $\square$

Remark — Doob's regularization theorem
This is the theoretical backbone of continuous-time stochastic analysis: it justifies assuming all martingales have RCLL paths, which makes $M_\tau$ well-defined, enables the Optional Stopping Theorem, and supports the Doob–Meyer decomposition and stochastic integration.
