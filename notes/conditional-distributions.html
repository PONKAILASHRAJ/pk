Conditional Distributions via Kernels

A Measure-Theoretic Treatment
Stochastic Processes  ·  Prerequisites: Measure theory, Radon–Nikodym

1. Motivation: Why Naive Conditioning Fails

The Core Problem
In elementary probability: \[ \mathbb{P}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap B)}{\mathbb{P}(B)}, \quad \mathbb{P}(B) > 0. \] But for a continuous $Y$ and any fixed $y \in \mathbb{R}$: \[ \mathbb{P}(Y = y) = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \mathbb{P}(X \in A \mid Y = y) = \frac{0}{0} \;\text{--- undefined!} \]
Concrete Illustration
$Y \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$, want $\mathbb{P}(X > 0.5 \mid Y = 0.3)$. Naive formula gives $0/0$. The fix: condition on regions, not points, and extract the pointwise information via Radon–Nikodym.
Historical Note
Resolved by Kolmogorov (1933). The regular conditional distribution — a Markov kernel $\{K(y,\cdot)\}_{y\in T}$ — is the modern solution.

2. Setup and Notation

Probability Space and Random Variables
Let $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ be a probability space. Let $(S,\mathcal{S})$ and $(T,\mathcal{T})$ be measurable spaces. Define measurable maps $X:\Omega\to S$ and $Y:\Omega\to T$.

Goal: Construct the distribution of $X$ given $Y=y$ for every $y\in T$.

Key Measures
Joint measure on $S\times T$: \[ \mu(A\times B) := \mathbb{P}(X\in A,\;Y\in B). \] Marginal of $Y$: \[ \nu(B) := \mathbb{P}(Y\in B) = \mu(S\times B). \]

Intuition: $\mu$ = full joint picture; $\nu$ = shadow of $Y$ alone. The kernel "lifts" $\nu$ back to $\mu$ by specifying how $X$ sits above each $y$.

3. The Four-Step Construction

Step 1 — Slice the Joint Measure

Fix $A\in\mathcal{S}$. Define the slice measure: \[ \mu_A(B) := \mu(A\times B) = \mathbb{P}(X\in A,\;Y\in B). \] $\mu_A$ is a sub-probability measure on $(T,\mathcal{T})$ with total mass $\mathbb{P}(X\in A)\le 1$.

Step 2 — Absolute Continuity

Claim: $\mu_A \ll \nu$.

Proof: $\nu(B)=0 \Rightarrow \mathbb{P}(Y\in B)=0$. Since $\{X\in A,Y\in B\}\subseteq\{Y\in B\}$, \[ \mu_A(B) \le \mathbb{P}(Y\in B) = 0. \] Hence $\mu_A\ll\nu$, and Radon–Nikodym applies.

Step 3 — Radon–Nikodym

There exists $k_A:T\to[0,\infty)$ with

\[ \mathbb{P}(X\in A,\;Y\in B) = \int_B k_A(y)\,\nu(dy). \]

$k_A$ is unique $\nu$-a.e. and plays the role of the "conditional probability density of $\{X\in A\}$ given $Y=y$".

Step 4 — Assemble the Kernel

Set $K(y,A):=k_A(y)$. Verification:

  1. $y\mapsto K(y,A)$ measurable for each $A$  (Radon–Nikodym).
  2. $A\mapsto K(y,A)$ is a probability measure for $\nu$-a.e. $y$  (Monotone Class).
  3. $K(y,S)=1$ $\nu$-a.e., since $\int_T K(y,S)\,\nu(dy)=\mu(S\times T)=1$.

$K$ is the regular conditional distribution of $X$ given $Y$.

What Makes a Markov Kernel?
A Markov kernel $K:T\times\mathcal{S}\to[0,1]$ satisfies: (i) $A\mapsto K(y,A)$ is a probability measure for all $y$; (ii) $y\mapsto K(y,A)$ is measurable for all $A$. It encodes a family of distributions — one per starting point $y\in T$.

4. Regular Conditional Probability (RCP)

Definition — Regular Conditional Probability
Let $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ be a probability space and $\mathcal{G}\subseteq\mathcal{F}$ a sub-$\sigma$-algebra. A regular conditional probability (RCP) given $\mathcal{G}$ is a function \[ \mathbb{P}(\,\cdot\mid\mathcal{G}) : \Omega \times \mathcal{F} \;\longrightarrow\; [0,1] \] satisfying:
  1. (Kernel) For each fixed $\omega$, the map $A\mapsto\mathbb{P}(A\mid\mathcal{G})(\omega)$ is a probability measure on $(\Omega,\mathcal{F})$.
  2. (Version) For each fixed $A\in\mathcal{F}$, the map $\omega\mapsto\mathbb{P}(A\mid\mathcal{G})(\omega)$ is a version of $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A\mid\mathcal{G}]$, i.e. it is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable and \[ \int_G \mathbb{P}(A\mid\mathcal{G})(\omega)\,\mathbb{P}(d\omega) = \mathbb{P}(A\cap G), \quad \forall G\in\mathcal{G}. \]
RCP vs. Conditional Expectation
The abstract conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A\mid\mathcal{G}]$ is only a random variable defined a.s. — it satisfies the integral identity but is not required to be a probability measure jointly in $A$.

An RCP is a simultaneous version: for a.e. $\omega$, the map $A\mapsto\mathbb{P}(A\mid\mathcal{G})(\omega)$ is a genuine probability measure. This is the extra regularity that makes the object useful for computation.

Theorem — Existence of RCP on Standard Borel Spaces
If $(\Omega,\mathcal{F})$ is a standard Borel space and $\mathcal{G}\subseteq\mathcal{F}$, then a regular conditional probability given $\mathcal{G}$ exists. Moreover, if $\mathcal{G}=\sigma(Y)$ for a measurable $Y:\Omega\to T$ (with $T$ standard Borel), then the RCP is given by the Markov kernel $K$ via \[ \mathbb{P}(A\mid\mathcal{G})(\omega) = K(Y(\omega),\, A), \quad \mathbb{P}\text{-a.s.} \]
Warning — RCP Can Fail on Non-Standard Spaces
On general measurable spaces, an RCP need not exist. A classical counterexample (Dieudonnè, 1948): let $\Omega=[0,1]$ with the $\sigma$-algebra of sets that are either countable or co-countable, equipped with a non-separable measure. The conditional probabilities exist as individual random variables but cannot be assembled into a kernel simultaneously for all $A$. This is why the standard Borel assumption is not just a technicality.
Definition — RCP Given a Random Variable
When $\mathcal{G}=\sigma(Y)$ for $Y:\Omega\to T$, the RCP simplifies to: a Markov kernel $K:T\times\mathcal{F}\to[0,1]$ such that for all $A\in\mathcal{F}$, $B\in\mathcal{T}$, \[ \mathbb{P}(A \cap \{Y\in B\}) = \int_B K(y,A)\,\nu(dy), \quad \nu = \mathbb{P}\circ Y^{-1}. \] We write $\mathbb{P}(A\mid Y=y) := K(y,A)$ as shorthand.
Theorem — Uniqueness of RCP
Any two regular conditional probabilities $K$ and $K'$ given $\sigma(Y)$ satisfy \[ K(y,A) = K'(y,A) \quad \text{for } \nu\text{-a.e. } y, \quad \forall A \in \mathcal{F}. \] Hence the RCP is unique in the $\nu$-a.e. sense.
Theorem — Factorization / Sufficiency
If $Z$ is $\sigma(Y)$-measurable (i.e. $Z = h(Y)$ for some measurable $h$), then for any $X\in L^1$: \[ \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]. \]

Interpretation: Once $Y$ is known, no additional $\sigma(Y)$-measurable information can change the conditional distribution of $X$. In statistics, this is the definition of $Y$ being a sufficient statistic.

Theorem — Conditional Independence via Kernels
$X \perp\!\!\!\perp Z \mid Y$ (read: $X$ is conditionally independent of $Z$ given $Y$) if and only if \[ K_{XZ|Y}(y,\, dx\,dz) = K_{X|Y}(y,dx)\otimes K_{Z|Y}(y,dz) \quad \nu\text{-a.e.} \] That is, the joint conditional kernel factorizes into the product of the marginal conditional kernels.
Chapman–Kolmogorov Equation
If $K_1:S\times\mathcal{T}\to[0,1]$ and $K_2:T\times\mathcal{U}\to[0,1]$ are Markov kernels, their composition is \[ (K_1\circ K_2)(x,C) = \int_T K_2(y,C)\,K_1(x,dy). \] This is the Chapman–Kolmogorov equation. For a Markov chain with one-step kernel $K$, the $n$-step kernel is the $n$-fold composition $K^n$. RCPs compose exactly this way, making kernels the natural language for Markov processes.

5. Main Definitions

Conditional Law
\[ \mathcal{L}(X\mid Y) := K(Y,\cdot) \]
A random probability measure: depends on $Y$, equals $K(y,\cdot)$ on $\{Y=y\}$.
Conditional Probability
\[ \mathbb{P}(X\in A\mid Y) := K(Y,A) \]
A random variable satisfying the tower law: \[ \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{P}(X\in A\mid Y)\,\mathbf{1}_{Y\in B}] = \mathbb{P}(X\in A,\,Y\in B). \]
Conditional Expectation via Kernel
For bounded measurable $f:S\to\mathbb{R}$: \[ \mathbb{E}[f(X)\mid Y] = \int_S f(x)\,K(Y,dx). \] Verification (tower law): \[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\int_S f\,dK(Y,\cdot)\cdot\mathbf{1}_{Y\in B}\right] = \int_B\!\int_S f(x)\,K(y,dx)\,\nu(dy) = \int_{S\times B} f\,d\mu = \mathbb{E}[f(X)\mathbf{1}_{Y\in B}]. \]

6. The Disintegration Theorem

Disintegration
\[ \mu(dx,dy) = K(y,dx)\,\nu(dy). \]
  • $\nu(dy)$: marginal of $Y$ ("how thick is each slice").
  • $K(y,dx)$: fiber distribution of $X$ above $y$ ("texture of each slice").

Integrate the fiber distributions against the marginal to recover the joint.

Recovering Everything from the Disintegration
\[ \mathbb{P}(X\in A,\,Y\in B) = \int_B K(y,A)\,\nu(dy). \] \[ \mathbb{P}(X\in A) = \int_T K(y,A)\,\nu(dy) = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\mathbb{P}(X\in A\mid Y)\bigr]. \]

7. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Discrete $Y$
$Y\in\{1,2,3\}$, $\mathbb{P}(Y=k)>0$. Then \[ K(k,A) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(X\in A,\,Y=k)}{\mathbb{P}(Y=k)}. \] The kernel reduces to the elementary formula; the RCP exists trivially. Disintegration = law of total probability.
Example 2 — Joint Gaussian
$(X,Y)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)$ with $\Sigma=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$. \[ K(y,\cdot) = \mathcal{N}(\rho y,\;1-\rho^2). \] $\rho=0$: $K(y,\cdot)=\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ (independence). $|\rho|\to 1$: variance $\to 0$ ($Y$ determines $X$).
Example 3 — Signal + Noise
$X = g(Y)+\varepsilon$, $\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)\perp Y$. \[ K(y,\cdot) = \mathcal{N}(g(y),\sigma^2). \] As $\sigma^2\to 0$, $K(y,\cdot)\to\delta_{g(y)}$ (point mass).
Example 4 — Mixture Model
$Y\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)$; $X\mid Y=i\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu_i,\sigma_i^2)$. \[ \mathcal{L}(X) = (1-p)\,\mathcal{N}(\mu_0,\sigma_0^2)+p\,\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2). \] This is the disintegration formula integrated over $Y$.

8. Important Subtleties

Subtlety 1 — Kernel Defined Only A.E.
$K(y,A)$ is defined for $\nu$-a.e. $y$ only. Conditioning on a literal zero-probability event is still undefined; $\mathbb{P}(X\in A\mid Y=y)$ is shorthand for $K(y,A)$.
Subtlety 2 — Standard Borel Required
RCPs are guaranteed to exist only when $S$ is a standard Borel space. Non-standard spaces can yield pathological failures (Dieudonnè, 1948).
Subtlety 3 — Borel's Paradox
The kernel depends on the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $Y$, not just the value $y$. On $\mathbb{S}^2$, conditioning on a great circle gives different answers in different coordinate systems.

9. Connection to Conditional Expectation

Abstract Conditional Expectation
$Z=\mathbb{E}[X\mid\mathcal{G}]$ is the unique $\mathcal{G}$-measurable $L^1$ random variable with $\int_G Z\,d\mathbb{P}=\int_G X\,d\mathbb{P}$ for all $G\in\mathcal{G}$.
Kernels Unify All Conditional Computations
\[ \mathbb{E}[f(X)\mid Y=y] = \int_S f(x)\,K(y,dx) \quad\nu\text{-a.e.} \] Corollaries: tower law, linearity, Jensen's inequality, independence characterisation ($K(y,\cdot)=\mathcal{L}(X)$ iff $X\perp Y$).

10. Applications

Bayesian Statistics
Prior $\mathcal{L}(X)$, likelihood encoded in $K(\cdot,\cdot)$, posterior $K(y,\cdot)=\mathcal{L}(X\mid Y=y)$. Bayes' theorem is the disintegration.
Markov Chains
Transition kernel: $K(x,A)=\mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}\in A\mid X_n=x)$. The Chapman–Kolmogorov equation is a composition of kernels.
Generative Models in ML
VAE decoder: $K(z,\cdot)$ maps latent $z$ to data distribution. Diffusion models: forward/reverse processes are kernels $K_t$. Conditional GANs: generator defines $K(y,\cdot)$ implicitly.

11. Summary

Key Formulas
\[ \mu(dx,dy) = K(y,dx)\,\nu(dy) \qquad\text{(disintegration)} \] \[ \mathcal{L}(X\mid Y)=K(Y,\cdot),\quad \mathbb{P}(X\in A\mid Y)=K(Y,A),\quad \mathbb{E}[f(X)\mid Y]=\int f\,dK(Y,\cdot) \] \[ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\int f\,dK(Y,\cdot)\right]=\mathbb{E}[f(X)] \qquad\text{(tower law)} \]
Three-Step Recipe
  1. Slice: $\mu_A(B)=\mathbb{P}(X\in A,\,Y\in B)$.
  2. Radon–Nikodym: $\mu_A(B)=\int_B k_A(y)\,\nu(dy)$.
  3. Assemble: $K(y,A)=k_A(y)$ is the RCP.
